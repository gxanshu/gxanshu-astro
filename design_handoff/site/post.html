I Was Scared of Dying… Until I Learned How to Live

For a long time, the thought of dying didn't scare me because of what came after. It scared me because of what I hadn't yet done.

There's a particular kind of 3am silence you only notice once you've spent a night staring at the ceiling, counting the years you've probably got left. I did the math more times than I'd like to admit. Forty years, if I'm lucky. Maybe fifty. Then nothing. And for a while, that "nothing" was the loudest thing in my head.

The quiet terror

It wasn't the dying that bothered me — not really. It was the silence of the days I hadn't yet lived. The books I hadn't read. The code I hadn't written. The tiny, unnoticed moments I kept postponing because I assumed time was renewable, like a good deploy.

"It is not that we have a short time to live, but that we waste a lot of it." — Seneca, On the Shortness of Life

Seneca is not a gentle writer. He doesn't cushion it. He just points — again and again — at the way we squander hours on things that don't matter, then complain that the years are too few. Reading him felt like being slapped, politely, by a Roman senator from two thousand years ago.

Time is the only non-fungible asset

I work in engineering. We measure everything. Latency, throughput, memory pages, p99. And yet the one resource we refuse to put a dashboard on is the one we can never allocate more of. You can double your RAM. You can't double your life.

So I did a small thing. I opened a text file called today.md and wrote down three lines:

  • What did I actually do today?
  • Did I want to do it?
  • If today were my last, would I regret it?

The first few entries were humiliating. The honest answer to question three, most days, was yes.

The turn

It wasn't a clean moment. It was messy, slow, and took months. But one day I realized I'd stopped being afraid of the ending — because I was too busy being interested in the middle. I started shipping small things again. Finishing books. Calling my mother. Walking without headphones.

Fear of death is almost always fear of an unlived life. Fix the second and the first dissolves.

What I do now

I still open today.md. I still answer honestly. Some days the answer is no, I wasted it. That's fine. The file isn't a report card; it's a mirror. You can't get better at living without looking at it.

If you're reading this at 3am, staring at your own ceiling — hi. The math hasn't changed. But you can change what you do with the time still on the clock.

— Anshu, Rajasthan · Dec 2025
